Homework 7 — Analogy Between the Bernoulli Process and the Server Security Random Walk

Understanding the deeper mathematical structures behind simulations

In the previous homework, I simulated the Law of Large Numbers (LLN) using a Bernoulli process — independent trials with outcomes 0 or 1. In this one, the Server Security simulation represents a related but slightly more complex random process, where multiple attackers act as combined Bernoulli events.

Both experiments illustrate how discrete random processes converge to stable probabilistic patterns when repeated many times. However, the second model introduces cumulative interaction, combining Bernoulli events into a binomial structure that behaves like a random walk.

The bridge between them lies in how independent binary events (success/failure) combine: LLN focuses on average frequency, while the Random Walk focuses on cumulative outcome.

1️⃣ Similarities

  • Both rely on Bernoulli trials — independent binary outcomes.
  • Each trial has a constant probability \(p\) of success and \(1-p\) of failure.
  • With many repetitions, the outcomes stabilize and become predictable in a statistical sense.
  • Both processes can be described using the binomial distribution.

In the LLN simulation, the frequency of success converged to \(p\). In the Server Security simulation, the cumulative score evolves as a random walk driven by the same underlying Bernoulli structure.

2️⃣ Differences

  • The LLN simulation measures average relative frequency of success. Its main variable is \( \frac{S_n}{n} \), where \(S_n\) is the number of successes after \(n\) trials.
  • The Random Walk simulation sums the signed outcomes: \( +1 \) for success (server secure) and \( -1 \) for failure (breach). The main variable is \( X_n = \sum_{i=1}^{n} Y_i \), with \(Y_i \in \{+1, -1\}\).
  • The first process converges to a mean value (LLN), the second fluctuates around a probabilistic expectation that follows the binomial pattern.

Conceptually, LLN shows stability in proportions; the Random Walk shows variability in accumulation.

3️⃣ Mathematical Connections

Both experiments are mathematically rooted in the binomial theorem:

$$ (p + q)^n = \sum_{k=0}^{n} \binom{n}{k} p^k q^{n-k} $$

Each term corresponds to the probability of obtaining exactly \(k\) successes in \(n\) Bernoulli trials. The coefficients \( \binom{n}{k} \) come from Pascal’s Triangle, which organizes all possible combinations of successes and failures.

In the security model, each path of the random walk corresponds to one sequence of outcomes — its probability is proportional to \( p^k (1-p)^{n-k} \).

The binomial theorem links directly to our simulation probabilities — it explains why the histogram of final scores tends to match the theoretical binomial curve.

4️⃣ Combinatorial and Fibonacci Analogies

The combinatorial structure of these experiments goes beyond simple probability. The binomial coefficients \( \binom{n}{k} \) also appear in many counting problems, recursive algorithms, and even in the Fibonacci sequence through combinatorial identities.

For example, Fibonacci numbers can be expressed as sums of binomial coefficients:

$$ F_{n} = \sum_{k=0}^{\lfloor (n-1)/2 \rfloor} \binom{n - k - 1}{k} $$

This shows how combinatorics, recursion, and probabilistic models share the same mathematical DNA. In our context, the number of ways the server can “walk” to a specific final score is also given by a binomial coefficient.

Every random trajectory corresponds to a unique combination of successes and failures — in other words, to one line in Pascal’s Triangle.

5️⃣ Conclusion

Both simulations, the LLN and the Random Walk, describe how randomness behaves under repetition. One emphasizes stability of frequency, the other distribution of accumulated outcomes. Mathematically, both are governed by the binomial law and its combinatorial properties.

These analogies are valuable in cybersecurity: they demonstrate that even in systems exposed to random threats, the long-term behavior is not entirely unpredictable — it follows clear probabilistic patterns rooted in classical mathematics.

The Bernoulli process, the binomial coefficients, and Pascal’s Triangle are not just abstract ideas: they are the foundation of every statistical model of reliability and risk.
